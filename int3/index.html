Jan Ortelius

Oh hey there, you must not be from here… let me introduce myself.

My name is Jan Ortelius, and as of a few weeks ago I’ve been looking for a job as a printer or typesetter… or actually I really like the Officina Plantiniana.

It really is everything I’m looking for in terms of a job. I have a great preference for printing however, I do not mind as long as I can stay in the printing business like my late father. And let’s face it, there won’t be a better place in the world for a flemish lad to work than the Officina Plantiniana here in Antwerp.

THE BUSTLING CITY OF ANTWERP

Antwerp truly is a marvel in terms of location in the 16th century. See, Plantin did not just pick this location at random.

Antwerp is a geological masterpiece as it has everything a growing business needs to grow.

You might be wondering now what’s so great about this Flemish city that I need 2 sentences to describe its beauty. Well, what else but the Great Port of Antwerp as I like to call it.

The port is one of the busiest in Europe during our time, connected to the River Scheldt and opening up trade routes that stretched as far as I can see and beyond.

Plantin was a businessman, and when he thought about how to get all his equipment to his office...

Well, that would’ve been hard to do over land and taken a lot of time. And the Plantin that I know is not a man for just wasting time, you know..

But the port wasn’t just about convenience for moving heavy printing presses and materials; it also means easy access to markets.

Antwerp is a booming trade hub, full of merchants, scholars, and craftsmen who could appreciate the value of books. Think about it - a city where you could sell your print locally and ship them out internationally, all in one go? That’s a business dream!

The city being so bustling and alive with opportunity, it should be called the Golden Age of Antwerp. And Plantin in this time, I’ve been told, is like the Jeff Bezos of our time - whoever that guy is …

Polyglot Bible

Now I hear you thinking, what would he ever need all that workforce for? Well, one of the many achievements of Plantin is that he prints the largest Polyglot Bible.

He actually did this to get him out of some trouble and prove his loyalty to the Catholic king Philip II of Spain - but more about that later.

The bible actually goes by a few names: ‘The Plantin Polyglot’, ‘The Antwerp Polyglot’, ‘The Biblia Regia’ or ‘King’s Bible’ and it was written in 5 languages: Latin, Greek, Hebrew, Chaldean, and Syrian (technically Ancient Syrian for you).

The project was so big that he needed 4 years to finish it; well that’s also because he made 1200 paper copies of it!

That insane feat aside, let me marvel again at his beatiful machinery.

One of the great printing presses that Plantin has, I’ve come here to study it a couple of times - well I need to if I want to be a printer here you know.

PRINTING PRESS

PRINTING PRESS

I’ll let you in on how it works, and then after I want you to link some of the words I used to their corresponding stamps:

First the forme, locked up in the chase, is inked on the press with the ink balls or dabbers. The slightly moistened paper is then clamped between the frisket and the tympan. With a sudden movement it is brought into contact with the type. Finally, the printer (me in the future) removes the printed paper, which will then be hung up to dry.

Now that you know how it works, try to match the words with the correct stamp

In the case of a normal book 1250 sheets of paper can be printed on both sides in a day on a single press, which means a printer has to perform 2500 impressions, which is like a whole lot... so an assistant usually helps the printer for this. So I’ll most likely start as an assistant first but not to worry, I have a plan to get me to the top spot in no time...

Christophe Plantin

Now that you know why I like the working place, how about I tell you all about my future boss huh?
The man is a legend.

When he first started he was just another printer trying to make a name for himself. But over the years, he’ grown his influence quite a lot. You might wonder how he did that, well I could write a whole book about all his achievements but let me do my best to summarise the big points to you before you run away. He has a great knack for spotting opportunities. He secured exclusive contracts with the Catholic Church to print religious text (like the Biblia Regia I mentioned before) and like you can imagine, that’s a gold mine during this great Catholic era.

But here’s the interesting thing - Plantin’s reputation has not always been that good sometimes.

The times he got in trouble

HERESY

He actually got in trouble a couple of times, the first time something happened was when he was suspected of heresy. They found a Calvinist pamphlet in his print shop, obviously the Catholics did not appreciate this but luckily he was away on a business trip at the time.

Then followed a whole ordeal in him getting back all his presses and stuff but like I said to much goes on for me to tell that part of his story you’ll have to check that out yourself when you go to his place…

FLEEING

Then the second time he got in trouble was when Plantin’s partners who actually were Calvinists, fled to the Netherlands this could have been seen as treason and they could’ve had his head.

A LETTER

Then there was the time where he started operations in Leiden.

He decided to go there in case the city would get captured by the Spanish governor at the time, but then when he tried to come back to Antwerp some people were against it. This is because after all he came from a place with lots of Calvinists so people thought he was Calvinist - but that was not true.

He had to get a letter from the king and then he showed it off to the people to let them know “Hey look, the king approves of me coming back to Antwerp”. He of course had the connection after being the Arch-printer to the king of Spain.

'BURN'

Then another time he got in trouble was when the Spanish Fury was unleashed in Antwerp, they even threatened to burn down the Officina Plantiniana.

BAD PERIODS

So that sums up the periods that would be bad to be an employee, but other than that I think he did fine. I don’t mind printing whatever comes my way, as long as Plantin stays true to God, I will work for him. So far, God does not seem angry, so why should I doubt his faith?

Jan Moretus

Okay, now here’s a little secret I found out: if you really want to secure your future at the Officina Plantiniana, you might want to pull a Jan Moretus. If you haven’t heard of him, he’s the guy who married Plantin’s daughter (Martina Plantin) and, in doing so, secured his job and is now set to inherit the entire business someday.

Now I’m not saying you should get married just for the job - that would be too much. But clearly connections matter Moretus didn’t just marry into the family; he proved himself worthy as a worker first. He learned the trade, worked his way up, earned Plantin’s trust, then just sealed the deal with a ring. He was just an opportunist and devoted to the company, trust me, Plantin would not let just anyone become the next heir.

Although it would be nice to marry my way into this grand entreprise, I want to earn my spot next to them.

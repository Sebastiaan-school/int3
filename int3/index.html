Polyglot Bible

Now I hear you thinking, what would he ever need all that workforce for? Well, one of the many achievements of Plantin is that he prints the largest Polyglot Bible.

He actually did this to get him out of some trouble and prove his loyalty to the Catholic king Philip II of Spain - but more about that later.

The bible actually goes by a few names: ‘The Plantin Polyglot’, ‘The Antwerp Polyglot’, ‘The Biblia Regia’ or ‘King’s Bible’ and it was written in 5 languages: Latin, Greek, Hebrew, Chaldean, and Syrian (technically Ancient Syrian for you).

The project was so big that he needed 4 years to finish it; well that’s also because he made 1200 paper copies of it!

That insane feat aside, let me marvel again at his beatiful machinery.

One of the great printing presses that Plantin has, I’ve come here to study it a couple of times - well I need to if I want to be a printer here you know.

PRINTING PRESS

I’ll let you in on how it works, and then after I want you to link some of the words I used to their corresponding stamps:

First the forme, locked up in the chase, is inked on the press with the ink balls or dabbers. The slightly moistened paper is then clamped between the frisket and the tympan. With a sudden movement it is brought into contact with the type. Finally, the printer (me in the future) removes the printed paper, which will then be hung up to dry.

Now that you know how it works, try to match the words with the correct stamp

In the case of a normal book 1250 sheets of paper can be printed on both sides in a day on a single press, which means a printer has to perform 2500 impressions, which is like a whole lot... so an assistant usually helps the printer for this.
